Remember how to perform long multiplication?

$$
\begin{array}{r}
  23 \\
\times 32 \\
\hline
  46 \\
+ 69\phantom{0} \\
\hline
  736
\end{array}
$$

This is the exact same algorithm we will use to multiply binary numbers. Here's how we would multiply $2 \times 3$.

$$
\begin{array}{r}
0010 \\
\times \quad 0011 \\
\hline
0010 \\
0010\phantom{0} \\
0000\phantom{00} \\
0000\phantom{000} \\
\hline
00000110
\end{array}
$$

All we have to do is convert decimals into their binary representation, then multiply as usual, keeping in mind that $0$ multiplied by anything gives $0$, and we only get a $1$ when it's $1 \times 1$.

Here's $3 \times 3$ in binary.

$$
\begin{array}{r}
0011 \\
\times \quad 0011 \\
\hline
0011 \\
0011\phantom{0} \\
0000\phantom{00} \\
0000\phantom{000} \\
\hline
00001001
\end{array}
$$

In binary, we must remember that $1 + 1 = 0$ carry $1$.

$$
\begin{array}{r}
0011 \\
\times \quad 0011 \\
\hline
00\underline{1}1 \\
00\underline{1}\underline{1}\phantom{0} \\
0000\phantom{00} \\
0000\phantom{000} \\
\hline
0000\underline{100}1
\end{array}
$$

Here we have $1 + 1 = 0$ carry $1$. Then $0 + 1 + 0$ plus the carried $1$ gives $0$ carry $1$. Then $0 + 0 + 0 + 0$ plus the carried $1$ gives $1$.

### How Computers Actually Do It

The algorithm that computers use _mimics_ this method. Say we want to multiply $0010 \times 0011$. We call $0011$ the "multiplier" and $0010$ the "multiplicand". The result is called the "product."

Since we are dealing with $4$-bit numbers, each multiplication operation takes $4$ iterations.

Here's the general recipe:

1. The product is set to $0$.
2. Check the least significant bit (LSB)—the rightmost bit—of the multiplier.
3. If it is a $1$, add the multiplicand to the product. If it is a $0$, nothing gets added.
4. Shift the multiplicand $1$ bit to the left.
5. Shift the multiplier $1$ bit to the right.

Let's trace $0010 \times 0011$ with this recipe.

Iteration #0

- Product is set to $0$.

Iteration #1:

- LSB of multiplier is $1$ ($001\boxed{1}$).
- Product = Product + Multiplicand = $0000 \ 0000 + 0000 \ 0010$ = $0000\ 0010$
- Shift left Multiplicand: $0000\ 0010 \rightarrow 0000\ 0100$
- Shift right Multiplier: $0011 \rightarrow 0001$

Iteration #2:

- LSB of multiplier is $1$ ($000\boxed{1}$)
- Product = Product + Multiplicand = $0000\ 0010 + 0000 \ 0100$ = $0000\ 0110$
- Shift left Multiplicand: $0000 \ 0100 \rightarrow 0000 \ 1000$
- Shift right Multiplier: $0001 \rightarrow 0000$

Iteration #3:

- LSB of multiplier is $0$ ($000\boxed{0}$). No operation.
- Shift left Multiplicand: $0000 \ 1000 \rightarrow 0001 \ 0000$
- Shift right Multiplier: $0000 \rightarrow 0000$

Iteration #4:

- LSB of multiplier is $0$ ($000\boxed{0}$). No operation.
- Shift left Multiplicand: $0001 \ 0000 \rightarrow 0010 \ 0000$
- Shift right Multiplier: $0000 \rightarrow 0000$

### Wait... What's Going On?

Here's the general recipe again, with the intuition behind each step:

1. The product is set to $0$. We start from scratch.
2. Check the least significant bit (LSB)—the rightmost bit—of the multiplier.
3. If it is a $1$, add the multiplicand to the product. If it is a $0$, nothing gets added.

Why does this adding make sense? Here's $0010 \times 0011$ again.

$$
\begin{array}{r}
0010 \\
\times \quad 00\underline{1}\underline{1} \\
\hline
\underline{0010} \\
\underline{0010}\phantom{0} \\
0000\phantom{00} \\
0000\phantom{000} \\
\hline
00000110
\end{array}
$$

Notice that every time the LSB of the multiplier is a $1$, we write down the multiplicand ($0010$). This contributes the multiplicand—shifted by the current iteration amount—to the product. That's why we add. On paper, when the LSB is $0$, we just write down a row of $0$s, which contributes nothing to the product. That's why we skip the addition.

4. Shift the multiplicand $1$ bit to the left.

We shift because if we were doing it on paper, in the first iteration we add $0010$, but by the second iteration we are adding $00100$ instead of $0010$—which is just $0010$ shifted left by $1$.

5. Shift the multiplier $1$ bit to the right.

We shift the multiplier to the right by $1$ so that in the next iteration we can check the new LSB to see if it's a $1$ or $0$.
