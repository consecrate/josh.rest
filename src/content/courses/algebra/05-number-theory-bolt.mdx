# The Clock World

Number theory is the mathematics of the **modular world**â€”a world where numbers wrap around like hours on a clock.

When it's 10 o'clock and you wait 5 hours, you don't get 15 o'clockâ€”you get **3 o'clock**. This is modular arithmetic in action: $10 + 5 \equiv 3 \pmod{12}$.

## Modular Arithmetic: The Remainder Game

The expression $a \pmod{n}$ asks: *"What's the remainder when $a$ is divided by $n$?"*

$$47 \pmod{10} = 7 \quad \text{(because } 47 = 10 \times 4 + \mathbf{7}\text{)}$$

**The Last Digit Trick:** The last digit of any number is simply that number mod 10!
- $12345 \pmod{10} = 5$
- $999 \pmod{10} = 9$

## Negative Numbers: Adding Your Way Up

What about $-3 \pmod{7}$? We need a positive remainder between 0 and 6.

**The trick:** Keep adding $n$ until you're positive!

$$-3 + 7 = 4$$

So $-3 \equiv 4 \pmod{7}$.

**Intuition:** On a clock, going back 3 hours is the same as going forward 9 hours (on a 12-hour clock). Both land on the same position!

## The Superpower: Reduce First, Then Compute

Here's the magic that makes modular arithmetic so powerful:

$$(a + b) \pmod{n} = [(a \pmod{n}) + (b \pmod{n})] \pmod{n}$$
$$(a \cdot b) \pmod{n} = [(a \pmod{n}) \cdot (b \pmod{n})] \pmod{n}$$

**Translation:** You can reduce each piece first, then add/multiply, then reduce again. This keeps numbers small!

**Example:** What's $47 \times 83 \pmod{9}$?

Instead of computing $47 \times 83 = 3901$ then dividing:

$$47 \equiv 2 \pmod{9}, \quad 83 \equiv 2 \pmod{9}$$
$$47 \times 83 \equiv 2 \times 2 = 4 \pmod{9}$$

# The Division Ladder: GCD

The **Greatest Common Divisor** $\gcd(a, b)$ is the largest number that divides both $a$ and $b$.

## Euclidean Algorithm: The Elegant Descent

Instead of factoring (which gets hard for large numbers), use the **Euclidean Algorithm**â€”a beautiful recursive descent.

**The Rule:** $\gcd(a, b) = \gcd(b, a \bmod b)$

**Example:** Find $\gcd(48, 18)$

| Step | Division | GCD Reduction |
|------|----------|---------------|
| 1 | $48 = 18 \times 2 + 12$ | $\gcd(48, 18) = \gcd(18, 12)$ |
| 2 | $18 = 12 \times 1 + 6$ | $\gcd(18, 12) = \gcd(12, 6)$ |
| 3 | $12 = 6 \times 2 + 0$ | $\gcd(12, 6) = 6$ |

When the remainder hits 0, the **last non-zero remainder** is your GCD!

## Coprime: The Special Relationship

Two numbers are **coprime** (or relatively prime) if $\gcd(a, b) = 1$.

This means they share **no common factors** except 1.

**Examples:**
- $\gcd(15, 8) = 1$ âœ“ Coprime!
- $\gcd(15, 9) = 3$ âœ— Not coprime (both divisible by 3)

## LCM: The Other Side of the Coin

The **Least Common Multiple** is connected to GCD by a beautiful formula:

$$\text{lcm}(a, b) = \frac{a \times b}{\gcd(a, b)}$$

**Why it works:** The product $a \times b$ counts the common factors twice, so we divide by GCD to correct.

# Multiplicative Inverses: Division in Mod World

In regular arithmetic, every non-zero number has a multiplicative inverse: $5 \times \frac{1}{5} = 1$.

In modular arithmetic, we ask: *Does $a^{-1}$ exist such that $a \cdot a^{-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{n}$?*

## The Golden Rule

$$a \text{ has an inverse in } \mathbb{Z}_n \iff \gcd(a, n) = 1$$

**Intuition:** If $a$ and $n$ share a factor, then $a \cdot x$ will always be a multiple of that factorâ€”it can never equal 1!

**Example:** Does $3$ have an inverse mod $10$?

$\gcd(3, 10) = 1$ âœ“

Yes! And $3 \times 7 = 21 \equiv 1 \pmod{10}$, so $3^{-1} = 7$ in $\mathbb{Z}_{10}$.

## Finding Inverses: The EEA Method

The **Extended Euclidean Algorithm** finds $x, y$ such that:

$$ax + ny = \gcd(a, n)$$

When $\gcd(a, n) = 1$, we get $ax + ny = 1$, which means $ax \equiv 1 \pmod{n}$!

So $x \pmod{n}$ is the inverse.

**Example:** Find $5^{-1}$ in $\mathbb{Z}_{11}$.

$$11 = 5 \times 2 + 1$$
$$1 = 11 - 5 \times 2$$

So $5 \times (-2) \equiv 1 \pmod{11}$, meaning $5^{-1} = -2 \equiv 9 \pmod{11}$.

**Check:** $5 \times 9 = 45 = 44 + 1 = 4 \times 11 + 1 \equiv 1$ âœ“

## Modular Division

"Dividing by $a$" in mod world means "multiplying by $a^{-1}$".

$$\frac{b}{a} \equiv b \cdot a^{-1} \pmod{n}$$

**Example:** What's $\frac{3}{7}$ in $\mathbb{Z}_{11}$?

First find $7^{-1}$: Since $7 \times 8 = 56 = 55 + 1 \equiv 1 \pmod{11}$, we have $7^{-1} = 8$.

Then: $\frac{3}{7} \equiv 3 \times 8 = 24 \equiv 2 \pmod{11}$.

# Fermat's Little Theorem: The Power Reducer

How do you compute $7^{1000000} \pmod{13}$? 

You can't calculate $7^{1000000}$ directlyâ€”it has millions of digits! But Fermat's Little Theorem saves the day.

## The Theorem

If $p$ is **prime** and $\gcd(a, p) = 1$, then:

$$a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$$

This is the **reset button** for powers!

---

## Using FLT: The Three-Step Method

**Problem:** Compute $7^{1000} \pmod{13}$.

**Step 1:** Verify FLT applies.
- Is 13 prime? âœ“
- Is $\gcd(7, 13) = 1$? âœ“

**Step 2:** Apply FLT. Since $7^{12} \equiv 1 \pmod{13}$:

**Step 3:** Reduce the exponent mod $(p-1)$.
$$1000 = 12 \times 83 + 4$$
$$7^{1000} = (7^{12})^{83} \cdot 7^4 \equiv 1^{83} \cdot 7^4 \equiv 7^4 \pmod{13}$$

**Step 4:** Compute the small power.
$$7^4 = 2401 = 13 \times 184 + 9 \equiv 9 \pmod{13}$$

## FLT Bonus: Finding Inverses!

From $a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$, we can derive:

$$a \cdot a^{p-2} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$$

So **$a^{-1} \equiv a^{p-2} \pmod{p}$**!

**Example:** $5^{-1}$ in $\mathbb{Z}_7$:

$$5^{-1} \equiv 5^{7-2} = 5^5 \pmod{7}$$

$5^2 = 25 \equiv 4$, $5^4 \equiv 16 \equiv 2$, $5^5 = 5^4 \cdot 5 \equiv 2 \cdot 5 = 10 \equiv 3$.

Check: $5 \times 3 = 15 = 14 + 1 \equiv 1$ âœ“

## Euler's Theorem: The General Version

What if $n$ is **not** prime? Use Euler's Theorem:

$$a^{\phi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod{n} \quad \text{when } \gcd(a, n) = 1$$

Where $\phi(n)$ is **Euler's totient function**â€”the count of integers from 1 to $n$ that are coprime to $n$.

**Key formulas:**
- $\phi(p) = p - 1$ for prime $p$
- $\phi(p^k) = p^{k-1}(p - 1)$
- $\phi(mn) = \phi(m)\phi(n)$ when $\gcd(m, n) = 1$
